COMPREHENSIVE EXAM GUIDELINES

Objectives

The Ph.D. Comprehensive Examination is one of the requirements for a doctoral degree in the Learning Sciences program. The overall objective of the Comprehensive Examination is to ensure that Ph.D. candidates have sufficient knowledge in their area(s) of study and that they are able to:
	•	select relevant and current literature to inform their doctoral research
	•	conduct conceptual/theoretical literature reviews and provide a critical analysis and evaluation of those literatures
	•	select and describe relevant and current methodological approaches
	•	conduct a critical analysis and evaluation of the various methodologies in their field
	•	use appropriate academic language to communicate, both orally and in written form, their knowledge and understanding of the empirical literature
	•	use APA style

The Ph.D. Comprehensive Examination consists of a written component and an oral component. These two components serve important functions to certify that students are able to: (a) critically review the theoretical and empirical literature, (b) recognize gaps in current knowledge, theoretical frameworks, and methods, (c) provide the justification and context for their dissertation research, (d) write coherently and concisely, using appropriate academic language and conventions to synthesize the reviewed literature and express their ideas, and (5) orally communicate the literature (and the points above) in clear and coherent ways.

In addition to this certification function, the examination serves as preparation for the doctoral student’s dissertation by: (a) facilitating doctoral students’ development of a solid conceptual and methodological base for dissertation research, and (b) ensuring that students’ knowledge is sufficiently broad and conceptually structured to provide a foundation for their proposed research and future scholarly activities.

Therefore, the Ph.D. Comprehensive Examination has three specific objectives that assess the breadth and depth of a candidate’s knowledge in their field(s) of study:
	•	Assess a candidate’s ability to situate the problem(s) being studied in the dissertation research within the larger intellectual and research contexts in the field;
	•	Assess a candidate’s ability to critically evaluate research, review the research literature, and identify gaps or unsolved problems in the field;
	•	Assess a candidate’s ability to effectively communicate, evaluate, and articulate knowledge and research approaches in the field of study through writing and oral discussion.

The Comprehensive Examination does not explicitly include assessment of knowledge and skill in research design and data analysis, but successful candidates will demonstrate sufficient knowledge of research design and data analysis concepts relevant to their doctoral research via their syntheses and evaluations of extant literature.

As a general principle, the examination process is intended to enable valid and authentic assessment and contribute to a student’s preparation for the dissertation research. The supervisor is responsible for ensuring that the student reads and understands the procedures outlined in this document.

Comprehensive Examination Committee

Composition. The Comprehensive Examination Committee comprises two or three faculty members from the Learning Sciences program or external to the program/university, if they have expertise in the content area. The Comprehensive Examination Committee may subsequently serve as the whole or part of the dissertation committee, although this is not a requirement. Note: GPS requires a minimum of two individuals on a dissertation committee: the supervisor may not be on the committee as part of the review process but is involved in question development. It is possible to have additional members on the committee. 

Formation. To form the Committee, the student and supervisor identify potential committee members together. Once agreement is reached, the supervisor invites potential members to serve on the Comprehensive Examination Committee. Once potential members accept the invitation, the student completes the Comprehensive Examination Registration form, informs the Program Director, and files the form with the Program Coordinator.

Content and Format

To complete their comprehensive examination, each student (1) formulates questions to be answered, and (2) answers those questions in 50 pages or less (double-spaced, excluding references). After the formulation of questions (see details below), the writing phase involves three months of writing (or four months for students whose first language is not English). Questions to be answered for the comprehensive examinations should be relevant to the student’s doctoral work and should be formulated such that answering them will advance the student’s mastery of their field(s) and provide a strong foundation for their doctoral dissertation.

Articulating the comprehensive examination questions. The doctoral seminar provides opportunities for students to work towards articulating coherent, focused, and comprehensive questions for the examination. Students typically write 2-5 pages of rationale providing background for their questions and articulating why they are important and relevant to answer. Concurrent consultation with the supervisor will help the student to further sharpen the focus of the questions. Once the supervisor approves the questions, the student meets with the comprehensive exam committee to present the proposed comprehensive exam questions. The questions should be sent to the committee first, before discussion. After the discussion, the student will refine the questions, if needed. The committee must then approve the questions verbally or via email; their signing of the Exam Registration Form (described below) represents official approval of the questions. Following the committee members’ approval, the student completes the Learning Sciences Comprehensive Exam Registration Form, obtains the required signatures, and submits the completed form, which includes the questions, electronically to the Program email account (edpsych.education@mcgill.ca) and Program Director. The Program Coordinator places the signed form in the student’s file.

Registration. In addition to filing the question and signed form, the student must formally register for the Comprehensive Examination course (EDPE 708) in the term in which the exam is to be completed, in accordance with the University registration deadlines. For further instructions, see the Normal Timetable section below.

Assessment of the Comprehensive Paper

The Comprehensive Paper shall consist of a critical review of the research and scholarly literature in Learning Sciences pertaining to their dissertation research. The goal of the Comprehensive Paper is to situate the candidate’s research problem within the context of both theoretical and empirical literature. The paper shall: (a) demonstrate breadth and depth of knowledge and conceptualization of the area of focus, (b) present a critical evaluation of the research and identify gaps or unsolved problems in that area, (c) situate a research approach and problem within its larger intellectual and research context, and (d) demonstrate skill in effective academic writing.

The Comprehensive Paper shall not exceed 50 pages (double-spaced, excluding bibliography), the balance of which should be approximately 25 pages for the first question and 25 pages for the second question, not including the References sections for each question.

To pass the Examination, the student must achieve a rating of at least satisfactory on all sections of the scoring rubric (see below), which is determined after the Oral Defence. If any one committee member selects unsatisfactory in any one category, the student fails the Examination and must rewrite it.

Taking into consideration the timeline for the committee to provide feedback to the supervisor, once the Comprehensive Paper is submitted via email to the supervisor and committee members, the Oral Defence should be scheduled. The Oral Defence provides an opportunity for the student to discuss the ideas presented in the Comprehensive Paper in an interactive context, to respond to questions and issues raised by committee members, and to address weaknesses in the written component (e.g., lack of critique, limited methodological integration) identified by the committee. 

Normal Timetable for the Comprehensive Exam

	•	The student is introduced to the Comprehensive Exam process and prepares the initial question during the 2nd term (or the 1st Winter term of study) of the Advanced Research Seminar 2 (EDPE 705), in collaboration with peers, doctoral research seminar instructors, and the research supervisor. This topic is revisited in the 3rd term (or the 2nd Fall term of study) of the Advanced Research Seminar 3 (EDPE 706) to prepare students to complete the Comprehensive Exam in the following term.
	•	The writing and evaluation of the Comprehensive Examination is normally carried out in the second year of the Ph.D. program (i.e., when the student is registered as PhD3). This corresponds with the 4th term (or the 2nd winter term of study) of the Advanced Research Seminar 4 (EDPE 707).
	•	The Comprehensive Examination is normally completed before the defence of the dissertation proposal - the blueprint for conducting the dissertation research. 
	•	The final question is submitted to the Comprehensive Examination Committee for their input and approval.
	•	Once committee members approve the question(s), the supervisor ensures that the Learning Sciences Comprehensive Exam Registration Form is signed by all committee members.
	•	To gain access to registration, the student scans and submits the completed Learning Sciences Comprehensive Exam Registration Form electronically to edpsych.education@mcgill.ca.
	•	Once received, a program coordinator will provide an approval override so the student may register for EDPE 708 in Minerva. Students must register for the comprehensive exam in the term in which it is to be completed by the applicable University registration deadline.
	•	The student has three months to complete the paper. Students whose first language is not English are given an additional month to complete the task. This extended period is to give the student sufficient time to fully edit the written paper to conform to standards of English language expression and syntax, as well as to APA standards.
	•	During the period that the Paper is being written, the student is not allowed to discuss the contents of the Paper with anyone.
	•	Once the Paper is completed, the student submits the completed work electronically via email to the supervisor and committee members, with a copy to their program email: edpsych.education@mcgill.ca. This is important as it keeps a record of the official submission date.
	•	This will normally be the end of the winter term or in exceptional cases, another designated deadline three (3) months after the commencement of the task.
	•	Committee members provide feedback to the Supervisor within two weeks, not including holidays.
The Supervisor summarizes the feedback from committee members and highlights any aspect(s) that the student requires further elaboration to complement what has been done in the Paper. This written summary of formative feedback shall be sent to the student and the committee within one month following the submission of the Paper.
Within two weeks of receiving feedback from the committee, the Oral Examination must be scheduled.
A final decision of PASS or FAIL will be made immediately following the Oral Examination.
	•	In case of urgent health, familial or parental issues, students may apply for an official leave of absence, which may be granted in accordance with the Leave of Absence Policy outlined in the Graduate eCalendar (https://www.mcgill.ca/gps/students/progress/leave- vacation).

Evaluation Criteria

Comprehensive Paper and Oral Defense:

	•	Selection of the literature
	•	Grounding in theory pertinent to the area of research
	•	Critical review of pertinent research including methodology, analysis, comparison, and synthesis leading to identifying gaps and limitations
	•	Linking the reviewed research to a general research problem to the broader structure of the field
	•	Effectiveness of written and oral communication
	•	Ability to address questions and concerns, referring to pertinent literature

Procedures for Assessment

	•	Each committee member prepares an individual written evaluation and completed rubric of the Comprehensive Paper and sends this to the supervisor.
	•	The supervisor summarizes and integrates the evaluations and circulates the summary to committee members for approval.
	•	Once approved, the supervisor sends the written feedback to the student to allow them to prepare for questions or issues that arose in the evaluation of the Comprehensive Paper at the Oral Defence. Note: The scoring rubric is not sent to the student at this time; only the written feedback is provided. This is due to the fact that the final rubric scoring will occur after the Oral Defence. The Oral Defence should be scheduled within two weeks following receipt of the feedback.
	•	At the Oral Discussion, the student spends 20 minutes highlighting content from the Comprehensive Paper, and at that point can address questions or issues provided by the committee.
	•	The committee may then ask the student questions at the Oral Defence.
	•	Once the Oral Defence is complete, the committee engages in a closed discussion as to whether the decision is PASS or FAIL. If the written paper and oral discussion meet expectations, the grade will be a PASS. In this case, the Comprehensive Examination Completion form will be signed by all committee members and will be submitted electronically to the Program Coordinator by the Supervisor. The student must also submit the form to MyProgress.
	•	If the written paper and oral discussion do not meet expectations, the grade will be a FAIL. In this case, as per GPS guidelines <https://www.mcgill.ca/gps/students/comprehensive-exams>, the student will be allowed one repeat of the comprehensive examination and given a minimum of four (4) months and a maximum of six (6) months for the rewrite. Note: The student may submit the rewrite at ANYTIME before the 4-6 month deadline.
	•	After the first failure, a grade of HH (which designates “continuing”) will be recorded on the student’s transcript. The student must be informed in writing by the supervisor that they have failed the comprehensive and must be informed of conditions relating to a repeat of the examination, including the nature of the re-examination and committee membership, as well as the deadline for rewriting the comprehensive paper.
	•	Depending on the quality of the comprehensive examination paper, the program may also require that the student take an additional course or courses where the student has shown weakness on the comprehensive examination.


Students and supervisors are strongly advised to consult the PhD Comprehensives Policy in the eCalendar section on Graduate Guidelines and Policies: https://www.mcgill.ca/gps/students/comprehensive-exams
